What Are Debits and Credits?

Most students think: "Debit means take away, and credit means add."

But that's not always true in accounting!

In accounting: "Debit" and "Credit" just show which side of an account is affected -- the left side (debit) or the right side (credit).

Imagine a seesaw. On the left, you have Debit (Dr), and on the right, you have Credit (Cr).

DEBIT
CREDIT

Every time something happens in a business, the seesaw must stay balanced -- whatever you add on one side, you must match on the other.

Think of an Account Like a Jar 🫙

Each type of account is like a different jar. What happens to that jar depends on whether you're adding to it or taking from it -- and whether it increases with debits or with credits.

Account Type Debit Means Credit Means
🏠 Assets ADD to the jar TAKE OUT
💰 Liabilities TAKE AWAY ADD
🧑‍💼 Equity TAKE AWAY ADD
💸 Expenses ADD TAKE OUT
🤑 Income TAKE OUT ADD
So, debit ≠ always "increase", and credit ≠ always "decrease" -- it depends on the type of account.

Examples (with stories!)

🔹 Cash Account (Asset)

Mr. ROI receives ₦10,000 cash from a customer.

  • Cash is an asset -- more cash means Debit Cash!
  • The customer is paying -- it's income, so Credit Sales.
DR. Cash Account ₦10,000  
CR. Sales Account ₦10,000

🔹 Paying Rent (Expense)

Mr. ROI pays ₦5,000 for rent.

  • Rent is an expense, which increases with a debit.
  • He used cash or bank, which goes down -- credit.
DR. Rent Account ₦5,000  
CR. Bank Account ₦5,000

In Simple Words…

  • Debit is the left side of any account.
  • Credit is the right side of any account.
  • The effect of debit or credit depends on the type of account.
  • Every transaction affects at least two accounts -- one debited, one credited -- and they must always balance.

Comic Strip: Mr. ROI's Business Day

Morning: Starting the Day

Mr. ROI opens his shop and invests ₦50,000.

DR. Cash Account ₦50,000

CR. Capital ₦50,000

Asset (Cash) increases with DEBIT, Capital increases with CREDIT.

Noon: Business Activities

Mr. ROI sells products for ₦20,000 cash.

DR. Cash Account ₦20,000

CR. Sales ₦20,000

Asset (Cash) increases with DEBIT, Revenue (Sales) increases with CREDIT.

Afternoon: Paying Expenses

Mr. ROI pays ₦10,000 for rent.

DR. Rent Expense ₦10,000

CR. Cash ₦10,000

Expense (Rent) increases with DEBIT, Asset (Cash) decreases with CREDIT.

Evening: Closing Time

Mr. ROI takes ₦5,000 home for personal use.

DR. Drawings ₦5,000

CR. Cash ₦5,000

Drawings (reduction of equity) increases with DEBIT, Asset (Cash) decreases with CREDIT.

Debit & Credit Quick Reference

A handy guide for accounting students

Account Type Normal Balance Debit Effect Credit Effect Examples
Assets Debit Increase (+) Decrease (-) Cash, Bank, Equipment
Liabilities Credit Decrease (-) Increase (+) Loans, Payables
Equity Credit Decrease (-) Increase (+) Capital, Earnings
Revenues Credit Decrease (-) Increase (+) Sales, Services
Expenses Debit Increase (+) Decrease (-) Rent, Salaries

Remember: DEAD CLIC

Debits increase: Debit, Expense, Asset, Drawings

Credits increase: Credit, Liability, Income, Capital

Golden Rules

  1. Debit what comes in, Credit what goes out
  2. Debit the receiver, Credit the giver
  3. Every transaction has at least 2 entries
  4. Total Debits must equal Total Credits

Debit & Credit Fundamentals

Understanding the Basics of Accounting

What Are Debits & Credits?

  • Debit = Left side of account
  • Credit = Right side of account
  • Effect depends on account type
  • Every transaction must balance

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

  • Assets: What business owns
  • Liabilities: What business owes
  • Equity: Owner's investment + profits

Increases & Decreases

Debit increases: Assets, Expenses, Drawings

Credit increases: Liabilities, Equity, Revenue

Common Transactions

Buy supplies with cash:

DR. Supplies (Asset) / CR. Cash (Asset)

Make sales:

DR. Cash (Asset) / CR. Sales (Revenue)
